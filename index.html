Assignment 4 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Kyle Choi — k3choi@ucsd.edu

Proposition: Issuing Green Bond Reduces Climate Related Wildfire

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Cherry-pick years (2015–2021 only) · Score: -2
    I restricted the data to years where issuance and wildfire appear inversely correlated, hiding earlier counter-trends. This fully deceptive choice fabricates a clean downward wildfire pattern by omitting years that don’t fit the narrative.
  • Semantic color coding (green for bonds, red for wildfires) · Score: +1
    I used green to connote environmental benefit and red to signal danger. This honest design aligns with natural associations and helps the reader instantly distinguish the two series.
  • Scale wildfire count ×5 to dramatize fluctuations · Score: -1.5
    Multiplying wildfire counts by five exaggerates its visual prominence, making year-to-year changes look steeper. Although the data are real, this amplifies noise and misleads the eye about magnitude.
  • Y-axis labeled only “Billions of Dollars” · Score: -1
    By labeling the primary axis solely in dollars, I obscure that one of the filled areas represents wildfire counts. This invites confusion and downplays the fact that the two areas use different units.
  • Omit “disaster-level” qualifier on wildfires · Score: -1.5
    I failed to note that only wildfires meeting specific severity criteria (e.g. ≥10 fatalities) are plotted. This omission inflates perceived severity by implying the series covers all wildfire events.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Remove the right-hand y-axis entirely · Score: -2
    Deleting the secondary axis leaves readers with no scale for the wild fire graph, preventing any magnitude check. It’s a fully deceptive tactic that hides how large or small the metric actually is.
  • Semantic color coding (red vs. green) · Score: +1
    I used the same red/green scheme to maintain visual consistency and rapid comprehension. This is an earnest choice—colors remain meaningful and don’t distort the data itself.
  • Slanted title “Blank Bond” · Score: -1
    The title frames green bonds as ineffective before the reader sees any data. It’s a mild rhetorical device that biases interpretation, though it doesn’t alter the underlying numbers.
  • Add “Disaster-level Wildfire” definition in legend · Score: +1.5
    I explicitly note that the wildfire series includes only events classified as “disaster-level.” This over-explains and put emphasis on the level of severity.
  • Overlay a dramatic wildfire background image · Score: +0.5
    The photo heightens emotional impact without changing any data values. It’s a lightly earnest technique to strengthen memory and context, rather than to mislead numerically.

Final Reflection

Reflecting on the Design Process
Working purely for persuasion was both freeing and challenging. On one hand, I relished experimenting with color semantics, ROI calculations, and even a dramatic wildfire overlay to see how far I could push reader perception. On the other, I discovered that choosing the “right” slice of data (2015–2021) felt almost too easy. Although it guaranteed a clean story, full transparency is overlooked. Deciding to highlight ROI appealed immediately, since the concept resonates with both finance professionals and the general public. Likewise, the wildfire background image tapped into visceral responses I’ve felt reading news reports. Yet slapping on a single axis label or omitting data qualifiers reminded me that small tweaks can profoundly reshape interpretation.

Defining Ethical Analysis & Visualization
I now see ethical visualization as a balance between clarity, honesty, and rhetorical power. True integrity means resisting the temptation to fit every chart to one’s preconceptions rather than cherry-picking years or erasing axes. I should ask myself: “Does this choice help readers understand the data, or merely persuade them?” Preprocessing steps like removing clear data errors or smoothing genuine noise can be an acceptable means of improving readability. By contrast, deleting entire date ranges, truncating units, or hiding an axis crosses into misleading territory because it actively obscures fundamental truths.

Acceptable Persuasion vs. Misleading Tricks
In practice, I’ve learned that many “earnest” techniques can also persuade effectively: meaningful color, transparent transformations, clear annotations. Even removing outliers, when done transparently, can sharpen focus without betraying the dataset. But tactics like cherry-picking, swapping units without notice, or erasing axis references offer no educational benefit. Going forward, I’ll embrace creative visualization choices that enrich understanding, while drawing the line firmly at any action that conceals or distorts the data’s full story.
